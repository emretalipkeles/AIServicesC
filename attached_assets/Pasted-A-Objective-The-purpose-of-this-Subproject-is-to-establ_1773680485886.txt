A. Objective
The purpose of this Subproject is to establish a consistent, repeatable, and defensible
methodology for identifying Contractor cause delay events on the J-Line project since
inception to date starting with issuance of Notice To Proceed on October 3 rd , 2024.
The aim of this exercise is to identify Contractor cause delay events in order to be used
for a future Delay Analysis in potential Claim Process.

B. Contractor-caused Delay
Contractor-caused delay, as referenced in AACE International Recommended Practice
No. 29R-03: Forensic Schedule Analysis (RP 29R-03, revised April 25, 2011), is a delay
event or period of delay attributable to the contractor (or parties for whom the contractor
is responsible, such as subcontractors or suppliers under their control). It is classified as
a non-excusable delay under the RP&#39;s framework for liability assessment
Contractor-caused delays fall squarely under non-excusable category, meaning they do
not entitle the contractor to an extension of time (EOT) or compensation, and they may
expose the contractor to liquidated damages (LDs) or other remedies if they impact the
critical path and extend project completion.
Potential Contractor caused delay events for J Line BRT Project including but not
limited to the below:
1 Planning and Mobilization Delays
1.1 Delayed mobilization of workforce or equipment to the site.
1.2 Failure to secure necessary subcontractor agreements in time.
1.3 Delays in obtaining contractor-required permits or approvals.
1.4 Mismanagement of project phasing, failure to shift resources where require to
effectively use remaining duration.
2 Labor-Related Delays
2.1 Staffing shortages or insufficient skilled labor for specialized tasks.
2.2 Low productivity due to inadequate training for workers.
2.3 Labor disputes or strikes within the contractor&#39;s workforce.
2.4 Failure to coordinate labor shifts, causing idle time during multi-phase works.
2.5 Inefficient crew allocation, such as assigning unqualified personnel.
2.6 Delays from subcontractor labor issues.
2.7 Poor management of labor resources during night shifts.
3 Materials and Equipment Delays
3.1 Delays in procuring materials, supply chain mismanagement.
3.2 Equipment breakdowns or shortages.

3.3 Late delivery of specialized equipment.
3.4 Material quality issues requiring reordering, such as non-compliant materials.
3.5 Failure to maintain equipment, leading to downtime.
3.6 Double handling of materials (if durations expand).
4 Subcontractor and Coordination Delays
4.1 Ineffective handoffs between trades, such as between civil crew and pavement
crew.
4.2 Failure to coordinate with subcontractors for timely completion of works.
4.3 Mismanagement of subcontractors for electrical tasks.
5 Quality and Rework Delays
5.1 Poor workmanship requiring rework that is already or can be an NCR.
5.2 Non-compliance with ADA standards for curb ramps or sidewalks,
necessitating corrections.
5.3 Defects in utility installations due to contractor errors.
5.4 Rework on electrical systems
6 Site Management and Safety Delays
6.1 Safety incidents or violations halting work on high-risk tasks.
6.2 Inadequate traffic control measures causing shutdowns.
6.3 Failure to mitigate minor weather impacts (e.g., not protecting exposed
subgrade), turning them into contractor delays.
6.4 Environmental non-compliance, such as improper handling of construction
debris from roadway excavation causing resources to be used to correct these
later.
7 Utility and Infrastructure-Specific Delays
7.1 Contractor errors in utility relocation or coordination with authorities for testing
or acceptance
7.2 Delays in subcontractor utility tie-ins ( need confirmation if under Contractor’s
scope of work)
7.3 Failure to protect existing utilities, causing utility strikes, damage and repairs.

D. Methodology
Contract 2023-002 Contract Plans, Volumes 1 through 4, will be uploaded to the project
environment to establish familiarity with the overall project layout, design intent, and
scope of work. These documents will serve as reference materials for understanding
work spatial constraints, and planned construction operations.
 Beginning in approximately Q2 2025 (anticipated around July 2025), Field
Inspectors were requested to explicitly identify potential Contractor-caused delay
activities in their Inspector Daily Reports (IDRs) using the designation
“CODE_CIE.” Each identified item will be tracked and mapped to the
corresponding CPM Schedule Activity IDs reflected as active in the applicable
monthly CPM update. This targeted approach is intentional, as it avoids
unnecessary review of activities that have not yet commenced or that were
already complete during the relevant update period.
 In parallel, all Non-Conformance Reports (NCRs) will be uploaded to the system
and similarly tracked against the CPM Schedule Activity IDs that were in
progress during the corresponding monthly schedule update. As with the IDR
review process, this methodology focuses exclusively on activities actively being
prosecuted during the relevant time period, rather than filtering or reviewing the
entire CPM schedule.
The IDR-based “CODE_CIE” entries and NCR records will form the backbone of the
initial dataset used to train the AI system, as these inputs provide clearer, more direct
indicators of potential contractor-caused issues. Each identified contractor-caused item
and the resulting AI output will be manually verified against contemporaneous source
documents to confirm accuracy and to ensure that no unsupported inferences or
hallucinations are introduced into the analysis.
Following this initial phase, Field Memos that includes documentation of observed
issues/problems at site and Differing Site Condition (DSC) claims that were reviewed
and NOT accepted by SDOT as contractor being responsible, will be incrementally
incorporated into the system. Additional project records deemed relevant for training
purposes may also be added. At each stage of data expansion, the AI outputs will be
reviewed manually, and the reasoning process will be evaluated to confirm logical

consistency, traceability to source documentation, and absence of hallucinated
conclusions.
Next, current IDRs for calendar year 2026 will be reviewed and processed to ensure
that ongoing conditions and contractor performance issues are accurately captured and
reflected in the dataset.
Finally, historical IDRs from project inception (October 2024) through July 2025 will be
uploaded to the AI system for retrospective analysis, applying the same verification and
validation procedures to ensure consistency, accuracy, and defensibility of the results.
